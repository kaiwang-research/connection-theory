# Feedback

> Feedback is the selective, history-dependent influence that decides which of a system's countless interactions leave a lasting mark on its organization - and, in doing so, lets an organization maintain persistent organizational continuity (Framework Principle 2) as conditions change.

## Definition

Feedback, within Connection Theory, is the history-dependent, selective influence of past interactions on a system's future organization.

A system undergoes an enormous number of interactions over its lifetime, yet only a small fraction leave any lasting trace on future behavior. Feedback names the criterion that separates the two: which past interactions continue to influence future organization, and which pass through without lasting effect. It is a criterion for *which* interactions matter, not a rule for exactly how the retained influence updates the organization - that update rule is deliberately left open.

Crucially, whether an interaction is retained is not a matter of simple decay over time. It depends on whether the interaction is compatible with, or reinforced by, the system's existing state - a history-dependent selection, not a uniform fading of every trace at a fixed rate.

---

## Effective Feedback

Not every repeated input qualifies as feedback in this sense. The operative test is whether an input *modifies the system's future response to structurally related inputs*. An input that produces no such change - one the system responds to identically no matter how often it recurs - is interaction, not feedback.

The cleanest illustration is the contrast between a reflex and a learned response. The patellar (knee-jerk) reflex answers the same stimulus the same way indefinitely and leaves no lasting trace. Habituation and sensitization in *Aplysia*, by contrast, measurably and persistently change the strength of future responses to repeated stimulation. Both involve repeated interaction; only the second is effective feedback.

---

## Intuition

Persistent organizations do not simply act; they continuously respond to the consequences of their interactions - but selectively.

Living organisms regulate internal processes through biological feedback.

Neural systems continuously reshape their activity based on which patterns of sensory input recur and matter.

Human organizations retain the outcomes that prove consequential and let routine encounters pass.

Artificial intelligence systems update their parameters according to which observed signals bear on performance.

Feedback is what allows organizations to learn from experience rather than repeatedly producing the same behavior - precisely because it does not treat every experience alike.

---

## Why It Matters

Without feedback, a system would treat every interaction alike, accumulating undifferentiated change with no way to tell which encounters should shape it and which should not. It would rapidly lose any stable identity.

Feedback is what makes selective retention possible. By determining which past interactions continue to bear on future organization, it lets a system accumulate the specific changes that maintain and improve it while letting the rest pass through without lasting effect.

This selective retention is what underwrites learning, self-regulation, and long-term persistence.

---

## Role in Connection Theory

Feedback connects a system's interactions with its future organizational change. It transforms undifferentiated organizational process into selective, adaptive process, and provides the foundation for:

- Adaptation
- Learning
- Continuous organizational refinement

Feedback explains why some interactions leave a lasting influence at all. How that influence then becomes a *stable, standing relationship* - resistant to being overwritten by the next interaction - is taken up separately by Connection.

Within Connection Theory, persistent adaptation depends on continuous, selective feedback between organizations and reality.

---

## Examples

Examples of feedback include:

- Homeostatic regulation in living organisms
- Learning in neural networks
- Performance evaluation within organizations
- Reinforcement learning in artificial intelligence

Although implemented differently, each selectively modifies future behavior based on which previous interactions proved consequential.
