# Organizational Continuity

**Definition.** Organizational Continuity is the criterion that licenses treating an adaptive system as *the same* system across an interruption of its Organization. It holds across a Flow interruption if and only if an identifiable persistence substrate of the Connection layer remains in **unbroken, non-duplicated custody** throughout the interruption — custody sufficient to reconstitute a *causally continuous* instance of Organization once Flow resumes.

## The problem it answers

Organization (see [organization]) is a continuously coordinated integration of connections. By its own definition it does not operate while the processes that constitute it are interrupted. Yet adaptive systems are routinely judged to be "the same" system after exactly such an interruption:

- a computer is the same computer after a reboot,
- a desiccated tardigrade is the same organism after revival,
- a river is the same river after a seasonal dry-up ends.

Neither Connection nor Organization, as previously derived, specifies what makes the resuming system *numerically the same* rather than a new instance that merely resembles it.

## Why resemblance and duration are not enough

Two systems can share an identical Connection-layer encoding without being the same system: two bit-identical hard drives are not "the same drive." Duration does not rescue the criterion either — a drive rebuilt from a backup image is not numerically the same drive as the one it replaced, even though the encoded content is identical. Something beyond *resemblance*, and beyond *how long the encoding lasts*, is doing the work.

Process continuity fails even more sharply. Across every interruption examined below, Organization is strictly absent throughout — so continuity of the organizational *process* cannot be the criterion for continuity across an interruption.

## The criterion: unbroken, non-duplicated custody

What survives scrutiny is a criterion of *custody*, not of resemblance or process:

> Identity is preserved across an interruption when an identifiable persistence substrate — a single physical encoding, or a distributed complex of institutional, documentary, or genetic encodings — remains in **unbroken, non-duplicated** custody throughout, sufficient to reconstitute a causally continuous instance of Organization once Flow resumes.

Resumption must draw on *that specific substrate*, not merely on a substrate of the same type.

### The tolerance window

The interruption a system can survive is bounded by its persistence substrate. This window is **zero** for some substrates and measured in **decades** for others — but never infinite. Continuous existence, in this precise sense, is not the claim that Organization never stops; it is the claim that a system's identity can survive Organization stopping, *within the tolerance window set by its persistence substrate*.

## Canonical instantiation

The same pattern holds across otherwise unrelated systems:

- ischemia tolerance in neural, cardiac, and skeletal-muscle tissue,
- seasonal river desiccation,
- unpowered digital storage,
- tardigrade anhydrobiosis.

In each, Organization does not survive the interruption, yet the system is judged continuous with its earlier self *if and only if* an identifiable persistence substrate remained in unbroken, non-duplicated custody, and resumption required an active Flow event that reconstituted Organization from that specific substrate.

**Mitosis provides an independent check.** DNA replication duplicates the persistence substrate and splits custody between two daughter cells. The criterion therefore predicts — correctly — that neither daughter is judged continuous with the parent in the ordinary sense. The *non-duplicated* clause is load-bearing, not decoration.

## Relationship to Organization and Flow

Organizational Continuity does not weaken the claim (see [organization]) that Organization itself requires continuous Flow. It explains a different thing: why systems are nonetheless correctly regarded as persisting through interruptions that Organization does not survive.

## Status

Grounded in the canonical cases above, with mitosis as a discriminating check. Like the other derived concepts, this criterion has been *introduced* by identifying an explanatory boundary that resemblance- and process-based accounts cannot cross; it has not yet been subjected to a dedicated Failure-Driven Principle Validation pass (see [framework] and Transformation Theory). Its current standing is "canonically instantiated, not yet independently falsification-tested."
